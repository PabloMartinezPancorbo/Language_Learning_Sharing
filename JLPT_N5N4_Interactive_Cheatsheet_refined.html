How to use this page

This guide now keeps your original kana, kanji, and vocabulary content in place, and adds the higher value material learners often miss: official JLPT structure, pass thresholds, grammar patterns, particles, conjugation cues, reading and listening strategy, and a practical high score study blueprint for both N5 and N4.

The current JLPT does not publish an official exhaustive vocabulary, kanji, or grammar list, so the most honest way to prepare is to combine official test structure and sample materials with strong beginner and elementary coverage across all core patterns.

Important: JLPT N5 and N4 both require you to pass the overall score and the section minimums. A strong vocabulary section cannot fully rescue a weak listening section.

What the exam really tests

  • Fast kana recognition, not slow decoding.
  • High frequency vocabulary in context, not only isolated memorisation.
  • Grammar choice, sentence flow, and text level understanding.
  • Reading short messages, notices, schedules, and everyday passages.
  • Listening for task completion, key points, quick response, and natural phrasing.

What changes from N5 to N4

  • N5 is about survival level understanding of basic sentences and short texts.
  • N4 adds more connected text, plain forms, comparison, reasons, intention, experience, conditionals, and more natural listening speed.
  • The biggest jump is not just more words. It is sentence linking and following meaning across a short paragraph.
Step 1
Automate kana first
If kana takes effort, every later skill becomes slower. Hiragana and katakana should feel immediate, including dakuten, handakuten, and combination kana.
  • Read all kana aloud daily.
  • Drill confusion pairs such as シ/ツ, ソ/ン, ぬ/め, れ/ね.
  • Practice without romaji as soon as possible.
Step 2
Master core grammar before chasing rare words
Particles, verb endings, adjective forms, existence verbs, requests, permissions, comparisons, reasons, and common connectors are score multipliers across reading and listening.
  • Memorise with example sentences, not single line definitions.
  • Study positive, negative, past, and past negative together.
  • Learn polite and plain forms side by side by N4.
Step 3
Read for structure, not word by word
A high score comes from spotting topic, subject, time markers, reasons, contrast, and what the speaker or writer wants you to do.
  • Underline particles and tense cues.
  • Find who does what, when, and why.
  • Train with notices, schedules, emails, and dialogues.
Step 4
Use timed practice early
Many learners know enough Japanese to pass, but lose marks to slow reading, second guessing, and weak first pass listening.
  • Do section drills under exam timing.
  • Answer on first hearing when possible.
  • Review wrong answers by pattern, not only by question.

Four week N5 intensive

  1. Week 1: kana automaticity, particles, です/ます, numbers, days, time, common verbs.
  2. Week 2: adjective forms, ある/いる, location, て form basics, requests, permission, likes, wants.
  3. Week 3: notices, short readings, listening drills, counters, daily routines, shopping, transport.
  4. Week 4: timed mixed practice, weak area review, sample questions, romaji off.

Six to eight week N4 build

  1. Weeks 1 to 2: plain forms, sentence linking, たり, ている, つもり, 予定, ことがある.
  2. Weeks 3 to 4: conditionals, giving and receiving, potential, そう, かもしれない, ながら, し.
  3. Weeks 5 to 6: paragraph reading, quick response listening, information extraction, weak grammar repair.
  4. Final phase: official style timed practice and repeated review of errors by grammar pattern.

Reading strategy

  • Read the question first when possible, then scan for time, place, person, quantity, and reason markers.
  • Treat は, が, を, に, で, から, まで, と, も, より, しか, ので, けど as navigation signs.
  • For notices and schedules, search for constraints: date, fee, where to go, what to bring, exceptions, deadlines.
  • If one option repeats words from the passage but changes the meaning, it is often the trap.

Listening strategy

  • Before audio starts, predict the kind of answer needed: place, item, action, reason, time, or opinion.
  • Focus on turn points such as でも, じゃ, それで, だから, けど, やっぱり, and changes of plan.
  • Do not translate every word. Listen for the decision.
  • For quick response items, drill natural replies, not textbook dictionary meanings alone.

High score habits

  • Study in Japanese daily, even for 20 to 30 minutes.
  • Use spaced repetition for kanji and vocabulary, but attach each item to a sentence.
  • Shadow listening audio aloud to improve recognition and rhythm.
  • Keep one error notebook sorted by particle mistakes, conjugation mistakes, and similar vocabulary mistakes.
